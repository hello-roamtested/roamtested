Landing in China with the wrong eSIM means no Google Maps, no WhatsApp, no Gmail — and no easy way to fix it once you're inside, because the app stores and provider websites you'd need are themselves blocked. This guide covers what each major China travel eSIM promises, what it actually publishes in writing, and the specific things worth checking before you hand over your money.

## How we test

Every eSIM covered here is bought with our own money — no free review units, no sponsored placements. Each one goes through the same protocol:

1. **Install and activate** following only the provider's own instructions, from outside China, on Wi-Fi.
2. **App-by-app firewall check** on arrival, with no VPN running: Google Search, Maps, Gmail, WhatsApp (messages and voice), Instagram, YouTube, and ChatGPT — each recorded as works / doesn't work, with a timestamp.
3. **Speed tests at fixed locations** — three runs each, median reported, always noting city, exact spot, and whether we're on 4G or 5G.
4. **Read the fine print** on every "unlimited" plan: is there a published daily cap, what speed do you drop to, and is that policy visible *before* you pay?
5. **IP exit check** to see which country the traffic actually leaves from — this is what determines whether the firewall applies to you at all.

## The contenders

### Yesim — built for longer stays ⬖

Unlimited day-pass pricing (from $4.66/day up to $65.20 for 30 days) makes Yesim the natural pick for anyone staying more than a week or tethering a laptop, since hotspot is included by default at no extra charge. It runs 5G on China plans and offers a 30-day refund policy. The catch is transparency: Yesim confirms a fair-use policy applies and that speeds "may be throttled after heavy usage," but publishes neither the daily cap nor the throttled speed — so you can't plan around it.

### Trip.com eSIM — the structural outlier

Every other option here is a foreign roaming eSIM. Trip.com's is sold through **China Mobile, China Unicom and China Telecom** — the three official domestic carriers — which makes it a fully legal, non-grey-market product with essentially nationwide coverage. Traffic is nonetheless routed internationally, which is why blocked apps are reported to work without a VPN.

It also has the most flexible plan ladder of anything on this page: **1 to 365 days**, 0.5GB/day up to unlimited tiers, plus fixed packages from **1GB to 100GB**, starting at **$2.94** for 500MB over two days ($8.47 for two days unlimited). Installation is in-app with no QR-code scanning, no Chinese ID or local number needed, and the whole thing is **refundable any time before you install it** — genuinely rare in this category.

Two open questions we're testing: no fair-use policy is published before purchase, and although it's marketed as "ChatGPT available," at least one buyer reports ChatGPT still needing a VPN while Google, Instagram, Facebook and WhatsApp worked fine. Given that app access is 30% of our score, that distinction matters.

### NXTL — pay-as-you-go, no expiry ⬖

A $11.99 one-time eSIM purchase that includes $10 of usable balance, billed per GB thereafter, with credit that never expires. That model suits anyone visiting China repeatedly or using very little data — no bundle to waste. The trade-off is predictability: NXTL doesn't publish a China per-GB rate on its pricing page (it surfaces in the in-app calculator), so the true cost of a China trip is hard to know before you commit.

### TooSim — broad coverage, steep China pricing ⬖

180+ countries, easy QR activation, no eKYC. But its China pricing is the outlier in the wrong direction: $13.99 for 1GB and $117.99 for 20GB, roughly three to six times what Nomad or Yesim charge for comparable data. Fine as a backup if you already use it elsewhere; hard to justify as a first choice for a China trip specifically.

## What to know before you buy any China eSIM

**Install before you fly.** This is the single most common mistake. Provider websites, app stores and QR-code emails may all be unreachable once you're inside China. Download the app, install the eSIM profile, and log in while you're still on a network you trust — most plans only start counting validity when you first connect at destination anyway.

**"Works in China" means routing, not magic.** These eSIMs aren't defeating censorship; your data is roaming out through a gateway in another country, so the firewall's domestic rules simply don't apply to that traffic. This is also why a data-only travel eSIM behaves differently from a local Chinese SIM sold at the airport, which generally does sit behind the firewall.

**Your eSIM won't receive your bank's SMS codes.** Travel eSIMs are data-only. Keep your home SIM active (or keep dual-SIM enabled) if you need two-factor codes for banking, or you'll be locked out at the worst moment.

**Check the hotspot policy.** Tethering is where "unlimited" plans quietly break down. Some providers include it free, some restrict it, and the ones that throttle after a daily cap will hit you fastest if you're sharing to a laptop.

**Read the throttling fine print — or note its absence.** Of the providers here, most do not publish a specific daily high-speed cap for their unlimited plans. A plan that gives 2GB/day at full speed and then drops to 1 Mbps is a very different product from one that doesn't say. Absence of a published policy is itself information.

**Check device compatibility first.** You need an unlocked, eSIM-capable phone: iPhone XS or newer, recent Samsung Galaxy S/Z, Google Pixel 4 or later. Phones bought in mainland China often have eSIM disabled in firmware.

## FAQ

**Do I need a VPN in China if I have a travel eSIM?**
For data on the eSIM itself, generally no — the traffic routes outside the firewall. But the moment you connect a laptop to hotel Wi-Fi, or use any local network, you're back behind it. Most travellers end up wanting both. See our [VPN pricing comparison](/vpn-for-china/).

**Does WhatsApp work in China on these eSIMs?**
WhatsApp messages and calls are reported to work on all the providers here, because the traffic never touches the domestic internet. It will *not* work on hotel Wi-Fi without a VPN.

**Can I get a Chinese phone number with these?**
No. All the eSIMs on this page are data-only. If you need a local number for domestic apps or deliveries, that requires a physical Chinese SIM and your passport.

**What happens when an "unlimited" plan hits its cap?**
Speed drops, usually to something between 512 kbps and 1 Mbps — enough for messaging, painful for maps and video. The problem is that most providers here don't publish where that cap sits.

**Is buying a China eSIM legal?**
Yes. Trip.com's is sold directly through the three state carriers; the others are ordinary international roaming products. Neither is a grey-market workaround.
